# How Washington workers' comp shapes the WCMSA

Washington is one of four monopolistic state-fund jurisdictions. The Washington State Department of Labor and Industries (L&I) is the sole workers' compensation insurer for non-self-insured employers; self-insured employers operate on a parallel track. Pricing follows the Medical Aid Rules and Fee Schedules (MARFS), published annually by L&I under RCW Title 51. The 2025 MARFS, effective July 1, 2025, includes a dedicated Chapter 19 for pharmacy. Pharmacy reimbursement uses a subset of the Washington State Preferred Drug List supplemented by an L&I-specific wrap-around formulary; prescription drugs for initial state-fund claim visits are covered regardless of formulary status.

Settlement of state-fund claims runs through Claim Resolution Settlement Agreements (CRSAs). L&I drafts the CRSA, the parties sign, and it goes to the Board of Industrial Insurance Appeals (BIIA) for approval. Either party may revoke consent within 30 days of approval before the settlement becomes final.

The general Medicare Regional Office for Washington is the CMS Seattle Regional Office. WCMSA submissions go through the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor and are approved by CMS itself. The Regional Office handles broader Medicare program operations.

Two structural features set Washington apart from a generic state-name swap: the monopolistic-fund architecture, and the CRSA's open-medical retention. The first means there is no third-party carrier to reconcile against. The second means a CRSA exchanges future indemnity for a lump sum or structured payment while leaving medical benefits open. The WCMSA has to delineate carefully between the funds it allocates and the continuing medical obligations L&I retains.

# Washington-specific considerations our clinicians keep in view

**The monopolistic state-fund structure.** L&I is the sole insurer for the standard Washington claim. There is no carrier-side variation in formulary, fee schedule, or settlement procedure to reconcile. Self-insured employers operate alongside L&I and follow a parallel but distinct CRSA path with their own administrators. The WCMSA must be built against the appropriate track from the start; the procedural posture of a state-fund file differs from a self-insured-employer file even though the federal CMS process is the same for both.

**The CRSA settlement vehicle and its open-medical retention.** A CRSA exchanges future L&I indemnity benefits for a lump sum or structured payment, but it does not close future medical. Medical benefits remain open as an L&I obligation after the CRSA settles indemnity. For a WCMSA, this means the report has to delineate clearly between the funds allocated for Medicare-covered future treatment and the continuing L&I medical obligation. A WCMSA that fails to make this delineation explicit produces post-settlement confusion about who pays what.

**BIIA approval and the 30-day revocation window.** CRSAs are submitted to the Board of Industrial Insurance Appeals after both parties sign. The Board reviews and approves; a 30-day revocation window then runs during which either party may revoke consent. The WCMSA is a primary supporting document for the BIIA's review and has to read defensibly during the window. Documentation of the projection methodology is what the Board looks at when assessing whether the medical-side allocation is reasonable.

**MARFS Chapter 19 pharmacy and the L&I formulary.** The 2025 MARFS includes a dedicated pharmacy chapter. L&I uses a subset of the Washington State Preferred Drug List plus a wrap-around formulary to control reimbursement. Treatment guidelines and formulary status determine drug coverage. For a WCMSA, every projected drug must be checked against the current L&I formulary and the applicable Chapter 19 rules. Drugs covered without restriction on initial state-fund claim visits may face different rules in the post-settlement period.

**CRSA statutory eligibility constraints.** Not every Washington claimant is eligible for a CRSA. Statutory eligibility constraints govern which workers can settle indemnity through this vehicle, including age thresholds and claim status requirements. The WCMSA process is only relevant where the file is eligible for a CRSA in the first place. We confirm CRSA eligibility from the file before scoping the projection.

# Washington WCMSA: questions we answer

**Which Washington fee schedule applies to a WCMSA?**
The L&I Medical Aid Rules and Fee Schedules (MARFS) under RCW Title 51 are the pricing baseline for medical services in Washington workers' comp. The 2025 MARFS, effective July 1, 2025, includes a dedicated Chapter 19 for pharmacy with a PDL subset and an L&I wrap-around formulary. Our allocations cite the schedule directly and document the per-line treatment of pharmacy.

**How does the CRSA structure affect a Washington WCMSA?**
A CRSA exchanges future L&I indemnity benefits for a lump sum or structured payment while leaving medical benefits open. The WCMSA has to delineate clearly between funds allocated for Medicare-covered future treatment and the continuing L&I medical obligation. We document this distinction explicitly so post-settlement responsibility for treatment categories is clear on the face of the report.

**How does BIIA approval work and what does the WCMSA need to satisfy?**
After both parties sign the CRSA, it goes to the Board of Industrial Insurance Appeals for approval. A 30-day revocation window follows during which either party may revoke. The WCMSA is a primary supporting document and has to read defensibly during the BIIA review. Documentation of the projection methodology drives whether the medical-side allocation reads reasonable to the Board.

**How does the L&I formulary affect projections?**
Every projected drug is checked against the current L&I formulary and the applicable MARFS Chapter 19 rules. The PDL subset and the wrap-around formulary determine reimbursability, and projections document formulary status for each line. Initial state-fund claim visit coverage and post-settlement coverage may differ; the report flags any differences.

**Which CMS office reviews a Washington-domiciled WCMSA submission?**
WCMSA proposals from Washington are reviewed by the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor (currently Capitol Bridge LLC) and approved by CMS. The CMS Seattle Regional Office handles general Medicare program functions for Washington but is not the entity that issues WCMSA approvals.

**Does Project Medical work Washington files for both defense and plaintiff?**
Yes. We work for any party with a valid interest in the file: carriers, defense counsel, plaintiff counsel, lifecare planners, structured settlement professionals. The methodology does not change with the requesting party; the report has to read cleanly to whoever is on the other side of the table.
