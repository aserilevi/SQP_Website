# How North Dakota workers' comp shapes the WCMSA

North Dakota is one of four monopolistic state-fund jurisdictions. North Dakota Workforce Safety & Insurance (WSI) is the sole insurer for non-federal employers, and unlike Ohio or Washington, North Dakota does not have a meaningful self-insured-employer alternative; WSI is effectively the only carrier in the state. Pricing follows the WSI Medical Fee Schedule Guidelines, which include separate sub-schedules for medical providers, pharmacy, inpatient hospital, and DME. The 2025 medical provider conversion factor is $80.67 per RVU on a single-CF RBRVS basis. Pharmacy reimbursement is set at WAC plus 8% plus a $4.00 dispensing fee per item, with WAC data sourced from Wolters Kluwer Medi-Span; WSI contracts with LucyRx as its pharmacy benefit manager. Settlement is negotiated directly with WSI, since WSI is also the sole settlement counterparty.

The general Medicare Regional Office for North Dakota is the CMS Denver Regional Office. WCMSA submissions go through the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor and are approved by CMS itself. The Regional Office handles broader Medicare program operations.

A North Dakota WCMSA is structurally distinct from a private-carrier-state file in a specific way: there is no carrier-side variation, no negotiating with a private payer, and no third-party administrator whose policies need to be reconciled. WSI sits on every side of the file as administrator, rate-setter, formulary controller, and settlement counterparty. The WCMSA is built against WSI's own posture from start to finish.

# North Dakota-specific considerations our clinicians keep in view

**The monopolistic state-fund structure with no private-carrier alternative.** Other monopolistic-fund states have meaningful self-insured-employer parallel tracks. North Dakota does not. WSI is the carrier in nearly every covered claim, which means the WCMSA has only one set of policies to reconcile against: WSI's. There is no formulary variation, no fee schedule variation, no UR variation across carriers. The simplification is real, but it also means the report has to read cleanly to WSI specifically, since WSI is the only audience inside the state that matters for the file's mechanics.

**WSI as both administrator and settlement counterparty.** WSI administers the claim, sets the reimbursement rates, manages the formulary, and is the entity that signs off on settlement. There is no separate insurer or TPA whose interests diverge from the agency's. For a WCMSA, this means the report's reconciliation work is to WSI's record alone. The projection follows the treatment record WSI has authorized and uses WSI's own fee schedule as the cost basis.

**The four-year dormancy rule.** A North Dakota claim is presumed closed if WSI has not paid any benefit on it for four years. This dormancy rule can affect how ongoing medical benefit entitlement is established at settlement. For a WCMSA, an active treatment record matters: if a long gap in benefit payment has occurred, the claim's posture may need to be re-established before a future-medical projection is meaningful. The report flags any dormancy concern in the engagement record.

**The single-conversion-factor RBRVS schedule.** WSI publishes a single conversion factor for medical provider services on an RBRVS basis. The 2025 figure is $80.67 per RVU. The single-CF structure differs from Medicare's category-specific conversion factors and from multi-CF state schedules. WCMSA cost projections for North Dakota use the WSI conversion factor for the year of projected service, with the schedule edition documented on the projection.

**ODG-based treatment guidelines and prior authorization.** WSI uses ODG by MCG (formerly the Official Disability Guidelines) as its evidence-based medical treatment guideline framework. Treatment that falls outside ODG benchmarks requires prior authorization through WSI's utilization review process. For a WCMSA, the projection accounts for whether projected future treatment is guideline-conforming, since non-conforming treatment will face authorization friction post-settlement.

# North Dakota WCMSA: questions we answer

**Which North Dakota fee schedule applies to a WCMSA?**
The WSI Medical Fee Schedule Guidelines are the pricing baseline. WSI publishes separate sub-schedules for medical providers, pharmacy, inpatient hospital, and DME. The 2025 medical provider conversion factor is $80.67 per RVU on a single-CF RBRVS basis. Pharmacy is set at WAC plus 8% plus a $4.00 dispensing fee per item. Our allocations cite the appropriate WSI sub-schedule for each projected service.

**How does the monopolistic-fund structure affect a WCMSA?**
WSI is the sole insurer and the sole settlement counterparty. There is no private carrier with separate policies to reconcile, and the report is built entirely against WSI's own treatment record, fee schedule, and formulary. The simplification is real, but it also means the report has to read defensibly to WSI specifically, since WSI is the only counterparty inside the state.

**How does the four-year dormancy rule affect projections?**
A claim is presumed closed if WSI has not paid any benefit on it for four years. If a long gap has occurred, the claim's active posture may need to be re-established before a future-medical projection has meaning. We flag dormancy concerns in the engagement record and confirm the claim's status before building the projection.

**How does ODG affect what we include?**
WSI uses ODG by MCG as its treatment guideline framework. Treatment within ODG is presumptively reasonable; treatment outside ODG requires prior authorization. The WCMSA projects future treatment with the ODG status documented; non-conforming treatment is flagged with the documented authorization rationale or excluded.

**Which CMS office reviews a North Dakota-domiciled WCMSA submission?**
WCMSA proposals from North Dakota are reviewed by the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor (currently Capitol Bridge LLC) and approved by CMS. The CMS Denver Regional Office handles general Medicare program functions for North Dakota but is not the entity that issues WCMSA approvals.
