# How Ohio workers' comp shapes the WCMSA

Ohio is one of four monopolistic state-fund jurisdictions in the United States. The Ohio Bureau of Workers' Compensation (BWC) is the sole insurer for non-self-insured employers, and self-insured employers operate on a parallel track with their own administrative procedures. Pricing follows the Ohio BWC Medical Fee Schedule under Ohio Administrative Code Chapter 4123-6. Pharmacy reimbursement is set at AWP minus 15% plus a $3.50 dispensing fee; 2025 proposed rule amendments would cap non-sterile compound reimbursement at $100 and replace fixed compound dispensing fees with time-based fees. Settlement of accepted future medical liability runs through Form C-240, with BWC approval followed by a 30-day Industrial Commission of Ohio (IC) review window.

The general Medicare Regional Office for Ohio is the CMS Chicago Regional Office. WCMSA submissions, regardless of state, go through the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor and are approved by CMS itself. The Regional Office handles broader Medicare program operations.

What makes an Ohio WCMSA structurally different from a generic state-name swap is the monopolistic-fund architecture. There is no carrier-side variation to account for; BWC is the carrier, the rate-setter, the formulary administrator, and one of the two settlement counterparties. The IC supplies the second. The report has to read against a state-fund file, not a private-carrier file.

# Ohio-specific considerations our clinicians keep in view

**The monopolistic state-fund structure.** BWC is the sole insurer for the standard Ohio claim. There is no third-party carrier whose medical-management policies, formulary, or fee schedule needs to be reconciled. Self-insured employers operate alongside the state fund and follow a parallel but distinct path. The WCMSA must be built against the appropriate track from the start; a self-insured-employer file is procedurally different from a state-fund file even though the federal CMS process is the same for both.

**Condition-by-condition allowance.** BWC's reimbursement is limited to the conditions that have been formally allowed in the claim. Additional conditions requested via Form C-9 but not yet allowed are not reimbursable until and unless BWC allows them. For a WCMSA, this means the projection covers only allowed conditions. Pending C-9 requests, denied conditions, and conditions outside the allowance remain outside the projection scope, with the rationale documented on the face of the report so a reviewer can see what was included and why.

**The C-240 settlement process and dual BWC/IC oversight.** State-fund settlements require BWC approval via Form C-240, followed by a 30-day IC review window during which the IC may disapprove only for clear unfairness; if the IC does not act within 30 days, the settlement is approved by operation of law. A mandatory 30-day cooling-off period then runs before the settlement becomes final. The WCMSA has to fit cleanly into this dual-review process. The report attached to a C-240 needs to read defensibly to both BWC and the IC, since either side can object during its respective window.

**The fee schedule's three-tier structure.** BWC publishes separate fee schedules for physician services, hospital outpatient (OPPS-based), and ambulatory surgery centers. The applicable schedule depends on the setting of care. Projecting future treatment costs in a WCMSA requires matching each projected service to the correct BWC fee schedule tier. A projection that prices an outpatient procedure as a physician service understates exposure; the reverse overstates it. We map each projected service to its correct tier and document the methodology on the projection.

**Permanent Total Disability lifetime medical exposure.** PTD awards through the IC carry lifetime medical exposure that does not close at MMI and does not have a statutory cap. A WCMSA on a PTD case has to project realistically against that horizon. The report documents life expectancy methodology, accounts for ongoing condition progression where the medical record supports it, and reflects the carrier-equivalent obligation BWC retains for the duration.

# Ohio WCMSA: questions we answer

**Which Ohio fee schedule applies to a WCMSA?**
The Ohio BWC Medical Fee Schedule under Ohio Administrative Code Chapter 4123-6 is the pricing baseline. BWC publishes separate schedules for physician services, hospital outpatient (OPPS-based), and ambulatory surgery centers; the applicable schedule depends on the setting of care. Pharmacy reimbursement is set at AWP minus 15% plus a $3.50 dispensing fee. Our allocations cite the appropriate BWC tier for each projected service and document the methodology on the projection.

**How does the condition-by-condition allowance affect what we include?**
The projection covers only the conditions formally allowed in the claim. Pending C-9 requests, denied conditions, and conditions outside the allowance are not in the WCMSA. We pull the allowance record from the file and use it as the rationale for inclusions and omissions, so a BWC or IC reviewer can see why a course of care is or is not in the allocation.

**How does the C-240 process affect the WCMSA submission?**
A Form C-240 settlement requires BWC approval followed by a 30-day IC review window, then a 30-day cooling-off period before the settlement becomes final. The WCMSA must fit cleanly into both review windows. We structure the report so it reads defensibly to BWC and the IC, since either can object within its respective period.

**Does the WCMSA differ for self-insured employer claims?**
The medical projection methodology is consistent, but the procedural posture differs. Self-insured employers operate on a parallel track with their own administrative procedures, and the settlement and approval mechanics are not identical to the BWC state-fund process. The report is built against the controlling track for the file, with the procedural posture documented up front so a CMS reviewer can see which Ohio system the file is in.

**Which CMS office reviews an Ohio-domiciled WCMSA submission?**
WCMSA proposals from Ohio are reviewed by the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor (currently Capitol Bridge LLC) and approved by CMS. The CMS Chicago Regional Office handles general Medicare program functions for Ohio but is not the entity that issues WCMSA approvals.

**Does Project Medical work Ohio files for both defense and plaintiff?**
Yes. We work for any party with a valid interest in the file: carriers, defense counsel, plaintiff counsel, lifecare planners, structured settlement professionals. The methodology does not change with the requesting party; the report has to read cleanly to whoever is on the other side of the table.
