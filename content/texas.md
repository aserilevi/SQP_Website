# How Texas workers' comp shapes the WCMSA

Texas pricing follows the Texas Department of Insurance, Division of Workers' Compensation Medical Fee Guidelines under 28 TAC Chapter 134. DWC sets conversion factors annually using the Medicare Economic Index; the resulting rates are DWC-specific and may exceed or fall short of current Medicare rates depending on service category. For context, the 2025 office-setting surgery conversion factor is $70.18. Pharmacy follows AWP × 1.25 plus a $4.00 dispensing fee for generics, with the closed pharmacy formulary based on ODG Appendix A controlling which drugs are reimbursable without preauthorization. Disputed issues run through a Benefit Review Conference, then a Contested Case Hearing if unresolved, with proposed settlement agreements filed on DWC Form-25 and submitted to the DWC field office for approval.

The general Medicare Regional Office for Texas is the CMS Dallas Regional Office. WCMSA submissions, regardless of state, go through the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor and are approved by CMS itself. The Regional Office handles broader Medicare program operations. Our reports are written to read cleanly against both the WCRC's review and the Texas DWC record.

# Non-subscriber distinction

Texas is unique in the United States for allowing employers to opt out of the workers' compensation system. Approximately 28-30% of Texas private employers are non-subscribers; their injured workers pursue tort or ERISA-plan remedies, and the resolution path is distinct from the DWC workers' comp system. **WCMSAs apply only to actual Texas DWC workers' compensation settlements.** If the file is a non-subscriber injury claim, the WCMSA process does not apply, and the conversation is a different one. We are glad to talk through file posture before any engagement begins. The rest of this page is written for files on the DWC workers' comp track.

# Texas-specific considerations our clinicians keep in view

**The closed pharmacy formulary.** Texas operates one of the strictest closed pharmacy formularies in the country. The formulary is built on the ODG Appendix A "N" drug list, governed by 28 TAC § 134.530. Drugs not flagged as "N" are reimbursable without preauthorization within their indicated use. "N" drugs require preauthorization for any prescription, including refills, and the carrier may deny. For a WCMSA, every projected drug must be checked against current "N" status, and any "N" drug projected for ongoing use needs documented preauthorization or a clinical exception, since post-settlement reimbursability depends on formulary status.

**The Designated Doctor program.** Disputed medical questions on impairment rating, maximum medical improvement, return-to-work, and similar issues go to a Designated Doctor appointed by DWC under §408.0041 of the Texas Labor Code. The Designated Doctor's opinion carries significant weight in DWC proceedings and anchors the impairment income benefits exposure being settled. For a WCMSA, the Designated Doctor's findings on MMI, impairment rating, and ongoing care are inputs the projection has to reconcile to clinically. Where a case has multiple Designated Doctor exams over its life, the controlling opinion is documented in the rationale.

**The Health Care Network structure.** Texas allows employers and carriers to establish certified Health Care Networks (HCNs) under 28 TAC Chapter 10. Where an HCN is in place, non-emergency treatment must come from in-network providers; care outside the network is generally not reimbursable. For a WCMSA, the HCN bounds the clinician roster available for projected future care, and pricing must track the HCN's contracted rates where they apply. We confirm HCN status from the file and reflect it in both the clinical narrative and the cost lines.

**The 104-week MMI presumption.** Under §401.011(30), maximum medical improvement is the earliest of either the date of permanent improvement or 104 weeks after temporary income benefits begin. The 104-week date attaches by operation of statute even where the treating clinician has not formally assigned MMI. For a WCMSA, the projection horizon anchors to the controlling MMI date, and cases with long pre-MMI treatment periods may reach MMI by operation of the statute alone. The report documents which MMI determination governs.

**The DWC fee guidelines and conversion factors.** The Medical Fee Guidelines are administered under 28 TAC Chapter 134. Conversion factors are set annually by DWC using the Medicare Economic Index, but the resulting rates are DWC-specific. The WCMSA prices each projected service against the applicable DWC conversion factor for the year of projected use, and the report documents the schedule edition and conversion factor on the face of the projection.

# Texas WCMSA: questions we answer

**Which Texas fee schedule applies to a WCMSA?**
The DWC Medical Fee Guidelines under 28 TAC Chapter 134 are the pricing baseline for medical services in Texas workers' comp. Conversion factors are set annually by DWC using the Medicare Economic Index; the resulting rates are DWC-specific. Pharmacy follows AWP × 1.25 plus a dispensing fee, subject to the closed formulary's "N" drug controls. Our allocations cite the schedule directly and document the per-line treatment of pharmacy, with the rationale visible to the Texas payer reviewing the file.

**Does this service apply to non-subscriber injury claims?**
No. The WCMSA process applies only to claims that resolve through the Texas DWC workers' compensation system. Non-subscriber injury claims resolve through tort or ERISA-plan remedies and are not WCMSA-relevant. If you are not certain whether a file is on the WC track, get in touch and we can talk through file posture before any engagement begins.

**How does the closed formulary affect projections?**
Every projected drug is checked against current "N" status under ODG Appendix A. Drugs not flagged as "N" are reimbursable without preauthorization within their indicated use. "N" drugs require preauthorization for any prescription and may be denied; a WCMSA projecting "N" drugs documents the preauthorization posture or a clinical exception, since post-settlement reimbursability at Texas fee schedule rates depends on formulary status.

**How do Designated Doctor opinions affect what we include?**
Where a Designated Doctor has issued an opinion on a disputed medical question, the projection reconciles to that opinion on MMI, impairment rating, and ongoing care. We pull the Designated Doctor's findings into the rationale for what is in and out of the allocation, and we document which Designated Doctor exam governs where multiple have been conducted over the life of the case.

**Which CMS office reviews a Texas-domiciled WCMSA submission?**
WCMSA proposals from Texas are reviewed by the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor (currently Capitol Bridge LLC) and approved by CMS. The CMS Dallas Regional Office handles general Medicare program functions for Texas but is not the entity that issues WCMSA approvals.

Yes. The engagement adjusts to the requesting party: narrative emphasis, add-on selection (credentialed-allocator review, CMS submission filing, rush turnaround), and submission posture all flex to fit the file and the settlement strategy. We work with carriers, defense counsel, plaintiff counsel, lifecare planners, and structured settlement professionals; the projection methodology stays constant so the report holds up under review.
