# How California workers' comp shapes the WCMSA

California pricing follows the Official Medical Fee Schedule (OMFS), promulgated under Labor Code §5307.1 and Title 8 CCR 9789.10 et seq., administered by the Division of Workers' Compensation. OMFS is its own pricing universe; rates for physician services run materially above the Medicare fee schedule, with California-specific conversion factors and adjustments. Pharmacy follows the OMFS Pharmacy Fee Schedule, with physician-dispensed drugs billed against OMFS and OTC medications limited to 120% of documented cost. Settlement of accepted future medical liability runs through Compromise and Release (C&R) agreements, approved by a workers' compensation administrative law judge at the Workers' Compensation Appeals Board (WCAB).

The general Medicare Regional Office for California is the CMS San Francisco Regional Office. WCMSA submissions, regardless of state, go through the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor and are approved by CMS itself. The Regional Office handles broader Medicare program operations. Our reports are written to read cleanly against both the WCRC's review standards and what a California payer expects to see on the file.

California's distinguishing variables go beyond the pricing schedule. Three procedural systems shape what counts as defensibly future-medical care, and a report that does not reconcile to all three of them is going to come back with edits.

# California-specific considerations our clinicians keep in view

**The UR/IMR record.** Utilization Review (UR) under Labor Code §§4610 and 4610.5 is the carrier's gatekeeping process for proposed treatment. When a UR denial is appealed, Independent Medical Review (IMR) issues a binding determination that controls the same treatment question for twelve months. For a WCMSA, a UR or IMR denial that has become final is the reason a course of treatment cannot be projected as future medical at California reimbursement rates. We document the UR/IMR record on the file and reflect denied treatment categories in the rationale for what we include and what we do not.

**The Medical Provider Network (MPN).** When the employer or carrier has filed an MPN with DWC under §4616, non-emergency treatment must come from in-network clinicians. Treatment outside the MPN is generally not reimbursable. The MPN therefore bounds the clinician roster available to deliver projected future care, and pricing for that care has to track the MPN's contracted rates where they apply. We confirm MPN status from the file and walk the projection through the network constraint when one is in place.

**QME and AME opinions on impairment and apportionment.** Disputed medical-legal opinions on causation, permanent impairment, and apportionment go to a Qualified Medical Evaluator (QME) under §4060, or to an Agreed Medical Evaluator (AME) when both parties select one. AME opinions carry greater weight than QME opinions in WCAB practice. Apportionment under §4663 (post-SB 899) attributes permanent disability to its causes, including non-industrial causes; for a WCMSA, apportionment shapes the share of future-medical exposure attributable to the industrial injury, and the report has to reconcile clinically with the controlling QME or AME findings.

**The C&R settlement vehicle.** A Compromise and Release closes future medical and is the settlement form that triggers the WCMSA analysis. Stipulations with Request for Award leave medicals open and the WCMSA analysis does not apply to them. We work the C&R track, and we structure the report to document what the C&R is actually closing, with any related claim that remains open under stipulation called out separately.

**The DEU rating process and the underlying medical record.** Permanent disability ratings are processed through the Disability Evaluation Unit using the AMA Guides Fifth Edition and the 2005 PDRS schedule (or the rating schedule applicable to the injury date). The rating that anchors settlement value comes out of the QME or AME's medical-legal report run through DEU methodology. The WCMSA must reconcile to the same medical record the rating sits on; otherwise the projection drifts from the file the parties already agreed describes the worker's condition.

# California WCMSA: questions we answer

**Which California fee schedule applies to a WCMSA?**
The OMFS is the pricing baseline for medical services in California workers' comp, with California-specific conversion factors that run materially above Medicare physician rates. Our allocations cite OMFS directly and document any deviation, with the rationale visible to the California payer reviewing the file. Pharmacy follows the OMFS Pharmacy Fee Schedule, with the per-line treatment of physician-dispensed and OTC drugs called out where they apply.

**How does the UR/IMR record affect what we include?**
UR approvals and denials, and the IMR decisions that follow them, define what treatment is defensibly future-medical. A UR-denied or IMR-upheld treatment category that has become final is not a line we project at California reimbursement rates. We pull the UR/IMR history into the report and use it as the rationale for inclusions and omissions, so a California payer can see why a course of care is or is not in the allocation.

**What if the worker is in an MPN?**
We treat the MPN as a constraint on the projection. Where the MPN is governing, projected future treatment has to come from in-network providers and is priced accordingly. We confirm MPN status from the file and reflect it in both the clinical narrative and the cost lines.

**How does apportionment affect the future-medical projection?**
Apportionment under §4663 attributes permanent disability across causes, including non-industrial causes. A QME or AME finding of substantial non-industrial apportionment changes the industrial share of permanent disability and, by extension, the share of future-medical exposure tied to the industrial injury. The WCMSA reflects the industrial share the QME or AME has identified, with the reconciliation between the medical opinion and the projection documented on the face of the report.

**Which CMS office reviews a California-domiciled WCMSA submission?**
WCMSA proposals from California are reviewed by the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor (currently Capitol Bridge LLC) and approved by CMS. The CMS San Francisco Regional Office handles general Medicare program functions for California but is not the entity that issues WCMSA approvals.

**Does Project Medical work California files for both defense and plaintiff?**
Yes. We work for any party with a valid interest in the file: carriers, defense counsel, plaintiff counsel, lifecare planners, structured settlement professionals. The methodology does not change with the requesting party; the report has to read cleanly to whoever is on the other side of the table.
