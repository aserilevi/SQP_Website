# How New York workers' comp shapes the WCMSA

New York pricing follows the New York State WCB Medical Fee Schedule, administered by the Workers' Compensation Board under §13 of the Workers' Compensation Law. Pharmacy follows the WCB pharmacy rules: brand drugs at AWP minus 12% plus a $4.00 dispensing fee, generics at AWP minus 20% plus $5.00, all subject to the New York State Workers' Compensation Drug Formulary that took effect in March 2022. Settlement of accepted future medical liability runs through a Section 32 Waiver Agreement, reviewed administratively by the Board with a ten-day withdrawal window before the formal decision issues; payment is due within ten days of that decision.

The general Medicare Regional Office for New York is the CMS New York Regional Office. WCMSA submissions, regardless of state, go through the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor and are approved by CMS itself. The Regional Office handles broader Medicare program operations. Our reports are written to read cleanly against both the WCRC's review and the Section 32 record the Board sees.

What makes a New York WCMSA different from a generic state-name swap is the depth of the procedural infrastructure that controls what counts as future medical. The Medical Treatment Guidelines, the drug formulary, the Schedule Loss of Use distinction, and the New York permanency impairment guidelines each shape the projection. We reconcile the report to all of them.

# New York-specific considerations our clinicians keep in view

**The Medical Treatment Guidelines (MTGs).** Mandatory since December 2010 and refreshed most recently in May 2022, the MTGs set evidence-based treatment standards for major body regions: mid and low back, neck, knee, shoulder, carpal tunnel, non-acute pain, PTSD, and several others. Treatment that falls within the MTGs is presumptively reasonable and necessary. Treatment outside the guidelines requires a variance request through the Board's OnBoard system, which the Medical Director's Office approves or denies based on submitted documentation. For a WCMSA, the variance approval history is a primary input: an approved variance for a course of treatment is what makes that treatment defensibly future-medical, while a denied variance that has become final means the treatment cannot be projected at New York fee schedule rates.

**The drug formulary.** The NYS Workers' Compensation Drug Formulary classifies drugs as Phase A, Phase B, perioperative, or non-formulary. Phase A drugs may be dispensed without prior authorization within their indicated use; Phase B drugs and non-formulary drugs require carrier prior authorization. For a WCMSA, every projected drug must be checked against current formulary status, since reimbursability turns on the formulary classification. Controlled substances and specialty medications are particularly likely to require ongoing prior authorization, and the report documents the formulary status of each line.

**Schedule Loss of Use (SLU) versus classification cases.** SLU awards under §15(3) compensate permanent functional loss of scheduled extremities (arms, legs, hands, feet, fingers, toes) and certain other body parts; they pay a fixed weekly award for a statutorily defined number of weeks based on the percentage loss. The SLU track does not require a finding of total or partial disability and produces a different settlement pattern than a classification case. A classification case (non-schedule permanent partial disability or permanent total disability) involves a Loss of Wage Earning Capacity determination and an open or capped weekly indemnity stream. The WCMSA is relevant to both tracks because both can close future medical via Section 32, but the medical picture supporting an SLU case looks different from one supporting a classification case, and the projection has to capture the actual treatment need on the file.

**The 2018 NYS Permanency Impairment Guidelines.** New York moved off the AMA Guides for permanency, and the current 2018 New York State Guidelines for Determining Permanent Impairment and Loss of Wage Earning Capacity govern PPI ratings. These guidelines are New York-specific and produce ratings that will not match AMA Guides Sixth Edition outputs for the same condition. The WCMSA reconciles to the rating produced under the controlling NYS guidelines, since that rating is what anchors the indemnity exposure the parties are actually settling.

**The Section 32 record itself.** A Section 32 Waiver Agreement is the document that closes future medical. The Board's administrative review issues provisional approval first, with the ten-day withdrawal window built into the schedule to let either party walk before the formal decision goes final. The waiver and its supporting medical record are what the Board, the WCRC, and CMS will each look at; the WCMSA is the document that has to read cleanly when attached to all three. We document what the Section 32 closes and what, if anything, remains open under separate stipulation.

# New York WCMSA: questions we answer

**Which New York fee schedule applies to a WCMSA?**
The NYS WCB Medical Fee Schedule is the pricing baseline for medical services in New York workers' comp, with pharmacy reimbursement set against the WCB rules and the state drug formulary. Our allocations cite the schedule directly and document the per-line treatment of pharmacy, with the rationale visible to the Board reviewing the file.

**How does the MTG and OnBoard variance record affect what we include?**
The Medical Treatment Guidelines define what treatment is presumptively reasonable and necessary for the listed body regions. Anything outside the guidelines lives or dies through the OnBoard variance system. A variance approval for a course of treatment is what makes that treatment defensibly future-medical; a denied variance that has become final means it cannot be projected at New York fee schedule rates. We pull the variance history into the report and use it as the rationale for inclusions and omissions.

**How does the drug formulary affect projections?**
Every drug projected in a WCMSA is checked against current formulary status. Phase A drugs within their indicated use carry no prior authorization burden. Phase B and non-formulary drugs require carrier prior authorization, and any controlled substance or specialty drug projected in a WCMSA is documented with formulary status and the prior authorization posture, since reimbursability depends on classification.

**How do SLU and classification cases differ for WCMSA purposes?**
SLU and classification cases can both settle future medical via Section 32, but the medical picture and the indemnity structure are different. An SLU case turns on a percentage loss of a scheduled body part and pays a defined weekly award. A classification case turns on Loss of Wage Earning Capacity and produces a weekly indemnity stream. The WCMSA captures the treatment need on the file in either posture, with the report structured to make clear which track the case is on.

**Which CMS office reviews a New York-domiciled WCMSA submission?**
WCMSA proposals from New York are reviewed by the CMS Workers' Compensation Review Contractor (currently Capitol Bridge LLC) and approved by CMS. The CMS New York Regional Office handles general Medicare program functions for New York but is not the entity that issues WCMSA approvals.

**Does Project Medical work New York files for both defense and plaintiff?**
Yes. We work for any party with a valid interest in the file: carriers, defense counsel, plaintiff counsel, lifecare planners, structured settlement professionals. The methodology does not change with the requesting party; the report has to read cleanly to whoever is on the other side of the table.

**How fast can we expect a New York report?**

The commitment is written into the engagement letter. Rush turnaround, credentialed-allocator review, and CMS submission filing are available as add-ons selected at intake.
